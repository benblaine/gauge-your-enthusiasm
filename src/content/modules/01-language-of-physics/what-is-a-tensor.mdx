# What Is a Tensor, Really?

<WhimsyBreak character="fox" mood="contemplative">
The fox swirled his glass of plum brandy and stared at the city. "A tensor,"
he said, mostly to the fern, "is the kind of object that doesn't care which
way you tilted your head when you measured it. Everything else is just
*coordinates*."
</WhimsyBreak>

A vector is a thing — an arrow in space — and the *components* of that vector
are how it looks once you've picked a basis. Pick a different basis, the
arrow is unchanged, but the numbers describing it change in a very specific
way.

A **tensor** is what you get when you generalise that idea: a multilinear
object whose components transform under a change of basis according to a
fixed rule that involves the Jacobian (and its inverse) once for each index.

## Indices, up and down

We write tensor components with indices. Some live upstairs, some downstairs:

$$
T^{\mu\nu}{}_{\rho}
$$

Two contravariant (upper) indices, one covariant (lower) index. They tell you
how to raise the basis change properly when you switch from one coordinate
chart to another.

The classic test for "is this object a tensor?" is the **transformation law**.
A vector transforms as

$$
V'^{\mu} = \frac{\partial x'^{\mu}}{\partial x^{\nu}} \, V^{\nu}
$$

A covector (one-form) transforms with the *inverse*:

$$
\omega'_{\mu} = \frac{\partial x^{\nu}}{\partial x'^{\mu}} \, \omega_{\nu}
$$

A higher-rank tensor stacks these factors index-by-index. Stuff that *doesn't*
transform like this isn't a tensor; it's a coordinate-bound illusion. Christoffel
symbols, for instance, are *not* tensors — and that's what makes them
interesting.

## The Einstein summation convention

Repeated index, once up once down ⇒ implicit sum:

$$
A^{\mu} B_{\mu} \equiv \sum_{\mu=0}^{3} A^{\mu} B_{\mu}
$$

If you see two upper or two lower indices repeated without a metric in
sight, something is wrong (or you're working in a flat Cartesian space and
being lazy).

## Why physicists love them

Because the *equations* of physics shouldn't care which coordinates you used.
Express a law as `T^{...}_{...} = 0` for some tensor `T`, and you've made an
honest claim — one that survives a coordinate change unscathed. This is the
whole geometric thrust of GR and gauge theory.
