# Maxwell's Equations in Tensor Form

<WhimsyBreak character="robot" mood="confused">
The brass-plated robot stared at four equations. "Why are there *four*," it
beeped. "Why not one. Or zero. Why is anything?"
</WhimsyBreak>

In undergraduate notation, Maxwell's equations are four:

$$
\nabla \cdot \vec E = \rho/\varepsilon_0, \quad
\nabla \cdot \vec B = 0, \quad
\nabla \times \vec E = -\partial_t \vec B, \quad
\nabla \times \vec B = \mu_0 \vec J + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \partial_t \vec E
$$

In relativistic / tensor notation, they are **two**:

$$
\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 J^\nu, \qquad \partial_{[\alpha} F_{\beta\gamma]} = 0
$$

In differential-form notation, they are **two and a half lines**:

$$
dF = 0, \qquad d{\star} F = J
$$

The collapse is not cosmetic. It tells you Maxwell's theory has a **gauge
symmetry**, lives on a **principal U(1) bundle**, and that $A$ is a
*connection* — exactly the structure that GR has for spacetime and the
Standard Model has for the strong and electroweak forces.

## The Faraday tensor

$$
F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu
$$

is antisymmetric, with components

$$
F_{\mu\nu} = \begin{pmatrix}
0 & -E_x/c & -E_y/c & -E_z/c \\
E_x/c & 0 & B_z & -B_y \\
E_y/c & -B_z & 0 & B_x \\
E_z/c & B_y & -B_x & 0
\end{pmatrix}
$$

E and B are not separate fields: they are different views of the same tensor
seen by different observers.

## Why gauge invariance is the point

Substitute $A_\mu \to A_\mu + \partial_\mu \chi$ for any scalar $\chi$:

$$
F_{\mu\nu} \to \partial_\mu(A_\nu + \partial_\nu\chi) - \partial_\nu(A_\mu + \partial_\mu\chi) = F_{\mu\nu}
$$

The fields don't notice. So the potential $A$ is not directly observable…
except through the Aharonov-Bohm effect, where the *holonomy* $\oint A\cdot dl$
*is*. Module 9 returns to this scandal.
