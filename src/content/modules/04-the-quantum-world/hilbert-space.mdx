# States Live in Hilbert Space

<WhimsyBreak character="raccoon" mood="smug">
The raccoon set down his martini. "Quantum mechanics is just linear algebra
with feelings. The feelings are the *measurements*."
</WhimsyBreak>

The full grammar of QM in three slogans:

1. A physical state is a unit vector $|\psi\rangle$ in a complex Hilbert
   space $\mathcal H$.
2. An observable is a Hermitian operator $\hat A : \mathcal H \to \mathcal H$.
3. A measurement returns an eigenvalue of $\hat A$ with probability
   $|\langle a|\psi\rangle|^2$ (the **Born rule**).

That's it. The Schrödinger equation is just the time-evolution part:

$$
i\hbar \frac{d}{dt}|\psi\rangle = \hat H |\psi\rangle
$$

## The uncertainty principle is a theorem about commutators

For Hermitian $\hat A, \hat B$ with $[\hat A, \hat B] = i \hat C$, the
Cauchy-Schwarz inequality applied to $|\psi\rangle$ gives

$$
\Delta A \, \Delta B \ge \tfrac{1}{2} |\langle \hat C \rangle|
$$

For $[\hat x, \hat p] = i\hbar$, this collapses to the textbook
$\Delta x \Delta p \ge \hbar / 2$. **Note**: nothing about microscopes or
"disturbance during measurement." It's a property of vector spaces with
non-commuting operators.

## From Poisson brackets to commutators

The classical Poisson bracket $\{q, p\}_{\rm PB} = 1$ becomes the quantum
commutator $[\hat q, \hat p] = i\hbar$ via Dirac's quantisation rule

$$
\{f, g\}_{\rm PB} \longrightarrow \frac{1}{i\hbar}[\hat f, \hat g]
$$

This is the bridge that the Lagrangian/Hamiltonian framework of Module 2
laid down for us. Quantum mechanics inherits its skeleton from the
symplectic geometry of phase space.
