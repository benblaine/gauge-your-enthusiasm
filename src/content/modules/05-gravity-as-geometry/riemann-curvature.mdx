# Riemann Curvature

<WhimsyBreak character="fox" mood="contemplative">
"The thing about curvature," the fox said, watching a vector parallel-transport
itself around a closed loop and not quite return, "is that it's not a fact
about the surface. It's a fact about how *failing to come back* feels."
</WhimsyBreak>

The Riemann tensor is the failure of parallel transport around an
infinitesimal closed loop. If you transport a vector $V^\mu$ around a tiny
parallelogram with sides $\delta x^\alpha, \delta x^\beta$, you don't come
back to $V^\mu$ — you come back to

$$
\delta V^\mu = -R^\mu{}_{\nu\alpha\beta} V^\nu \, \delta x^\alpha \delta x^\beta
$$

That coefficient, $R^\mu{}_{\nu\alpha\beta}$, is the **Riemann curvature
tensor**. In coordinates,

$$
R^\rho{}_{\sigma\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu \Gamma^\rho_{\nu\sigma} - \partial_\nu \Gamma^\rho_{\mu\sigma}
+ \Gamma^\rho_{\mu\lambda} \Gamma^\lambda_{\nu\sigma} - \Gamma^\rho_{\nu\lambda} \Gamma^\lambda_{\mu\sigma}
$$

It's the commutator of two covariant derivatives, $[\nabla_\mu, \nabla_\nu] V^\rho = R^\rho{}_{\sigma\mu\nu} V^\sigma$.

## The hierarchy

Contract Riemann to get less information, more brevity:

| Object | Formula | Encodes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Riemann | $R^\rho{}_{\sigma\mu\nu}$ | Full curvature, 20 independent components in 4D |
| Ricci | $R_{\mu\nu} = R^\rho{}_{\mu\rho\nu}$ | Volume distortion sourced by matter |
| Scalar | $R = g^{\mu\nu} R_{\mu\nu}$ | Trace |
| Weyl | $C_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu}$ | Trace-free part: tidal / vacuum / GW |

In a perfectly homogeneous and isotropic universe (FLRW), the Weyl tensor
vanishes — there's nothing for tidal forces to lock onto.

## And then Einstein

$$
G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}
$$

$G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2} R g_{\mu\nu}$ is the Einstein tensor.
The Bianchi identity $\nabla_\mu G^{\mu\nu} = 0$ is automatic, which means
$\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0$ — local conservation of energy-momentum is *built
into the geometry*.

That coupling $8\pi G/c^4 \approx 2.08 \times 10^{-43}$ (SI) is the "G/c⁴
problem": warping spacetime by anything noticeable requires absurd amounts of
stress-energy. This is why building a wormhole is hard.
